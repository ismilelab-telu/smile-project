AI, machine learning, deep learning, and generative AI often appear close together. To keep them from feeling like a pile of jargon, we need to place them on one simple map.

**Artificial Intelligence**, or AI, is the broad umbrella for systems that perform tasks usually associated with human intelligence. Examples include recognizing patterns, understanding language, making decisions, planning actions, or helping solve problems.

**Machine learning** is one way to build AI. The difference is that an ML system does not only follow fixed rules. It adjusts its behavior from data and examples it has learned from.

## Term Map

Think of these terms as layers:

- **Artificial Intelligence**: the broad umbrella for systems that imitate or assist human thinking abilities.
- **Machine Learning**: a branch of AI that learns from data.
- **Neural Network**: one family of ML models that uses layers of artificial neurons.
- **Deep Learning**: neural networks with many layers, often strong for images, audio, text, and complex data.
- **Generative AI**: an AI area focused on creating new content such as text, images, audio, video, or code.

## Common Mix-Ups

Not all AI is machine learning. Rule-based systems can also be called AI if they are designed to solve certain intelligent tasks.

Not all machine learning is deep learning. Classic models such as linear regression, decision trees, and random forests are still ML even though they do not use deep neural networks.

Modern generative AI often uses deep learning, but its goal is specific: **creating new content**. Many other ML models focus on prediction, classification, recommendation, risk detection, or grouping.

## Practical Handle

The short map:

1. AI is the broad umbrella.
2. Machine learning is the part of AI that learns from data.
3. Neural networks are one family of ML models.
4. Deep learning means deeper neural networks.
5. Generative AI is an AI area that creates new content.

With this map, you can read AI terms without mixing everything into one same thing.
